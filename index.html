What is Satellite Internet?

Satellite internet uses orbiting satellites to relay data between a user terminal and the wider internet. A small dish or terminal talks to a satellite, which relays traffic to a ground station connected to the global network.

Typical components include the user terminal (dish or flat panel), satellites in orbit (LEO or GEO), and ground stations (gateways) that interface with terrestrial networks.

How it works

  1. User Terminal — your home or mobile antenna sends/receives signals.
  2. Satellite — relays the signal; LEO (low earth orbit) sit closer and lower latency, GEO (geostationary) sit far away and cover large areas.
  3. Ground Station — gateway that connects the satellite link to the internet backbone.
  4. Internet — data reaches services across the world and returns the same way.
LEO vs GEO (simple): LEO satellites orbit closer and can provide lower latency, while GEO satellites stay over one spot and cover wider areas but with higher latency.

Why it matters for India

  • Rural coverage where fibre doesn't reach.
  • Rapid deployment after natural disasters.
  • Connectivity for islands, border regions and remote schools.
  • Mobility for ships, trains, and aviation.
  • Network redundancy — backup to terrestrial links.

Benefits

Wide Coverage

Satellites can reach remote or sparsely populated regions more easily than laying fibre.

Quick Deployment

Services can be activated quickly, helpful after disasters or for temporary needs.

Backup Connectivity

Useful as a redundant path when terrestrial networks fail.

Mobility

Works on ships, trains, and in remote field operations where fixed networks can't.

Challenges

Latency

GEO links have higher latency than fibre; LEO reduces this but needs more ground infrastructure.

Weather Interference

Heavy rain or storms can reduce performance in certain frequency bands.

Equipment Cost

Terminals and installation can be more expensive than a SIM card or local Wi-Fi setup.

Data Caps & Regulation

Providers may limit data or be subject to local regulations and licensing.

India-specific Use Cases

  • Remote schools/telemedicine: Bringing education and medical consultations to villages.
  • Disaster relief: Quick comms after floods, earthquakes where ground networks fail.
  • Border & islands: Network for border posts and island communities.
  • Mobility: Trains, ships and aircraft staying connected during travel.
